Tesla · SolarCity · High-Consideration Retail · 2016–2018

The insight

Purchase decisions don't happen in isolation — they happen in moments

The consumer psychology behind a $100,000 vehicle purchase is not fundamentally different from any other retail decision — both are governed by the same forces: desire, trust, and friction. What's different is the timeline and the number of people involved.

At Tesla, we identified that the vehicle purchase journey had a moment — typically after the test drive, during financing — where the customer was emotionally committed but hadn't yet signed. That window was the best possible moment to introduce home energy. They were already in a "big decision" mental state. Adding solar and Powerwall to that moment reduced perceived friction because the customer was already thinking long-term.

The retail design problem was: how do you make that moment feel seamless rather than like a sales add-on? The answer was integration — physical merchandising, digital tools, and advisor training that made energy a natural part of the vehicle conversation rather than an upsell.

"The best upsell feels like the obvious next question, not a new conversation."

Act one · Tesla

Rebuilding the in-store experience around a single customer journey

When I joined Tesla, the in-store experience for vehicles and the solar/energy product line were operating as parallel tracks — different spaces, different advisors, different conversations. A customer could leave with a Model 3 having never heard about Powerwall, even if they would have bought it.

The redesign integrated physical merchandising — the Solar + Powerwall wall you see below — with digital tools that let advisors pull up a customer's home energy profile mid-vehicle conversation. Test drives were paired with energy consultations. Come-to-you drives included a home energy walkthrough. Online booking fed into a single CRM that treated vehicle and energy as one household decision.

Left: Tesla Solar + Powerwall retail display unit · Right: Tesla Solar in Home Depot (SMM 2018)
30%+ Energy / solar attachment rate on Model 3 purchases

A 30%+ attachment rate on a vehicle purchase for a product that requires a separate installation, separate financing, and a separate decision process is not a sales result. It's a design result. The experience created the conditions for that decision to happen naturally.

Act two · SolarCity

Scaling the same insight across 800 stores and a 1,500-person field org

SolarCity (acquired by Tesla in 2016) operated a massive direct sales operation — door-to-door, events, retail partnerships. The challenge was that CAC was high and quality was inconsistent. The same insight that drove the Tesla retail redesign applied here: the moment of maximum openness to a solar conversation is when a customer is already thinking about their home.

The Home Depot partnership put us at the exact right moment — a customer buying a water heater or HVAC system is already in a home-improvement mindset, already thinking about energy costs. The branded display in the lumber and garden departments wasn't incidental placement. It was precision timing.

I built and managed the 1,500-person field marketing and lead generation organization, designed the quality and conversion tracking system, and negotiated and operationalized the national retail partnerships — Home Depot, Best Buy, BMW, AT&T, DIRECTV. Each partnership was chosen for the same reason: access to a customer in the right moment of receptivity.

What came next

The through-line from Tesla to what followed

The Tesla and SolarCity chapters taught me something about how category-defining brands operate in the physical world — and how different that is from the digital-first thinking most brand leaders are trained in. The best consumer moments aren't engineered; they're discovered and then designed around.

That understanding — of how trust operates at the point of transaction, not just in the awareness funnel — is what made the next chapter possible. A parking technology company with no brand, no category, and a 1% market share turned out to be a direct application of what I learned trying to sell solar panels in a Home Depot lumber aisle.
