The category problem

Parking technology had no category leader and no brand language

When I joined FlashParking, the connected parking space was a real product category solving a real problem for cities and operators — but nobody had named it, positioned it, or built a brand around it. The competitors were pitching technology specs to procurement committees. The buyers were drowning in RFPs that all looked the same.

Government buyers — the cities and municipalities that own and operate parking infrastructure — are not irrational. They respond to the same trust signals that consumer buyers do: does this organization have a point of view? Does it understand my problem specifically, or is it pitching a generic solution? Does it look like it will still be here in five years?

Brand answers all three questions before the first sales call happens. The FlashParking go-to-market was built on that premise from day one.

"Government buyers don't check out of their humanity when they open an RFP. They still trust what feels trustworthy."

The B2G insight that generalized: procurement processes are filters, not decisions. The real decision happens before the RFP — when a buyer decides which vendors they want to win. Brand determines who gets on that shortlist.

What was built

Naming, positioning, and go-to-market architecture from zero

The first job was naming the category. "Parking technology" was descriptive but flat. "Connected mobility infrastructure" was accurate but jargon. We landed on language that centered the city's outcome — smarter, more efficient use of urban space — rather than the product's feature set. The brand voice followed: authoritative, civic-minded, built for buyers who manage public trust, not just procurement budgets.

The go-to-market architecture had to operate across three distinct buyer types simultaneously: cities and municipalities (B2G), parking operators (B2B), and the drivers who ultimately use the product (B2C). Each required a different channel mix, a different proof set, and a different trust signal. The brand platform had to work for all three without sounding like it was built for none of them.

I built the marketing function from scratch — team, agency relationships, content strategy, trade presence, and the earned media program that produced the IPMI cover. The earned media was not incidental. In a category where buyers read the trade press seriously, a cover story in Parking & Mobility is a sales tool.

The insight that generalizes

Government buyers respond to brand. The data says so.

The conventional wisdom in B2G sales is that government is purely rational — specs, price, compliance. That's wrong, and FLASH's trajectory proves it. We went from less than 1% market share to 33% in a market where our product was not the cheapest and our company was not the oldest.

The differentiator was brand clarity. When a procurement committee has to justify a vendor choice to a city council or a board of directors, they reach for the brand narrative as much as the spec sheet. "They're the ones who do this specifically for cities like ours" is easier to defend than "they had the lowest per-transaction fee."

We made it easy to choose us by making it easy to explain choosing us. That's brand doing its job in a B2G context.

In March 2022, FlashParking raised $250M in a strategic investment led by Vista Equity Partners and L Catterton, crossing a $1 billion valuation. At that point the company operated at 10,000+ locations and processed over $1 billion in annual transactions — serving 11 million parkers per month across every major U.S. city and Canada.
