Realtor.com · Brand Relaunch · 2023–2026

Rebuilding a challenger brand against a 10× outspend

Homes.com outspent us 10 to 1. Zillow outspent us 4 to 1. The question wasn't whether to respond — it was whether a 30-year-old brand had the credibility to win a trust war it couldn't win on volume. The answer turned out to be yes, but only after we stopped trying to compete on their terms.

The challenge

A brand assault nobody expected

Realtor.com had operated as the second-largest portal in real estate for years — with credibility tied to the NAR relationship and a user base built on MLS accuracy. Then Homes.com arrived with a 10× media budget and a simple message: your listing, your lead. Zillow, already at 4× our spend, doubled down on product.

The defensive play — match spend, chase features — wasn't available to us. We had to find a terrain where 30 years of heritage was the advantage, not a liability. That terrain turned out to be trust. Not trust as a tagline. Trust as the entire strategic frame: don't out-shout, out-clarify. The positioning we built — Expert Edge — was grounded in the insight that real estate is one of the few purchases people genuinely fear getting wrong. Not just financially. In front of their family, their partner, their life.

"The brands winning share aren't the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones consumers believe when it matters most."

The insight was hiding in the data. Realtor.com users had the highest intent of any portal — they were closer to transaction, more likely to actually buy or sell. The brand had earned trust it hadn't yet claimed in its creative.

The work

Reba McEntire and the business case for celebrity

Most brand teams treat celebrity as a media buy. We treated it as a strategic signal. The question we asked wasn't "who has reach?" — it was "who do people trust when they're making the most important decision of their financial lives?" Home buyers and sellers aren't just afraid of financial loss. They're afraid of embarrassment — of getting the biggest decision of their life wrong in front of the people they love. That emotion is FOSU: fear of screwing up. It's the real engine behind every high-stakes real estate transaction.

Celebrity done right is a trust shortcut. It transfers credibility from a face people already believe in to a brand they're still evaluating. Reba McEntire scored higher on trustworthiness than any other celebrity tested, and her identity — someone who navigated major life reinventions and came out stronger — mapped directly to what buyers and sellers feel. She wasn't amplifying our message. She was embodying our values.

The creative format — a fictional Reba-hosted show set in a familiar sitcom house — gave the brand warmth that most real estate advertising doesn't have. The coherence between who she is and what the brand stands for is what moves brand health over time, not just in the week of launch.

I owned the business case internally, locked Reba as the target, led the negotiation, and partnered with GSD&M to take the campaign from brief to national air. The internal agency I rebuilt — 30+ people with hires from YETI, USAA, Adobe, Expedia, and Indeed — handled production alongside the agency relationship.

Product launch

Realtor.com+: the brand and the product moving together

Realtor.com+ was a B2B product launch — a first-of-its-kind collaborative home search experience built for agents. Brand wasn't the vehicle here; it was the foundation. The Expert Edge positioning that was doing trust-building work with consumers was doing the same work with agents deciding which platform to invest in. Agents choose platforms the same way consumers choose brands: they go where they feel credible, not just where the features are best.

The launch required brand, product marketing, and performance to operate as a single motion — consistent positioning across an audience that was simultaneously a user, a customer, and a distribution channel.

Realtor.com+ — product launch

The press release framed it as a new category: collaborative home search. The go-to-market made it feel like a logical extension of what the brand had already built — not a pivot, not a feature announcement, but the next thing a brand with genuine expertise would build.

AI creative

Using AI to scale without losing the brand

The Seller AI campaign represented a different kind of creative challenge: how do you use AI-generated creative at scale without it feeling like a cost-cutting exercise? The answer was to treat AI as a production tool inside a brand system that was already doing the trust-building work — not as a substitute for the brand, but as a way to reach more sellers faster without diluting what the brand stood for.

AdExchanger covered the approach as an example of how legacy portals are rethinking their creative infrastructure — not just making cheaper ads, but building systems that let a brand position scale without decaying.

Thought leadership

The SXSW "Nearly Home" panel was an invitation-only gathering of senior marketing and advertising leaders.
